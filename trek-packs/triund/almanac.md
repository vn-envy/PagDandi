# Triund–Indrahar Trek Almanac

Curated offline knowledge for the Triund trail. Ships inside the Trek Pack; retrieved on-device by EmbeddingGemma. General guidance only — conditions change; verify on trail.

## Route stages and character

McLeod Ganj (2,082m) to Dharamkot junction is 1.3 km of village lanes and cafés. Dharamkot to Galu Temple (2,300m, 2.8 km mark) is a gentle warm-up on a motorable track. Galu to Magic View Café (2,520m, 5.1 km) climbs steadily through oak and rhododendron forest on a well-worn path. Magic View to Snowline Café (2,700m, 7.4 km) steepens with rocky switchbacks locals call "the 22 curves". The final push to Triund Top (2,875m, 9.2 km) opens onto the meadow ridge with the full Dhauladhar wall ahead.

## Water sources

The stream near Galu (2,280m) is the last reliable running water before the ridge; flow is strong in monsoon and spring melt. The Magic View spring (2,510m) is seasonal — dependable July–September, a trickle by late autumn, often dry May–June before the rains. There is no natural water at Triund Top itself; the cafés sell bottled water at a premium. Fill 2L per person at Galu on the way up.

## Weather by season

March–April: clear mornings, rhododendron bloom, snow patches above Snowline. May–June: haze builds by noon, hot lower sections, afternoon thunderheads possible. July–September (monsoon): daily afternoon storms, slick rock, leeches below 2,400m, but the greenest meadows. October–November: the classic window — crisp air, long views, cold nights near 0°C on top. December–February: snow above Snowline is normal; the top is often -5°C at night and the trail can need microspikes after storms.

## Afternoon wind and storms

The Dhauladhar wall generates strong anabatic winds; the exposed ridge above Snowline picks up gusts most days after 15:00. In pre-monsoon and monsoon months, cumulus over the Indrahar line by early afternoon is the cue to be off the exposed sections by 16:00. Lightning strikes on the open meadow are a documented hazard — if caught, drop below the ridge line, avoid lone trees.

## Flora — forest bands

The trail crosses three bands: ban oak and Himalayan cedar (deodar) to ~2,300m; burans — Rhododendron arboreum, Himachal's state flower, deep red bloom March–April — with kharsu oak to ~2,700m; then dwarf juniper, grasses and alpine herbs on the ridge. Locals make sharbat from burans petals. Trekkers must never eat or forage wild plants.

## Fauna and wildlife cautions

Himalayan Griffon vultures ride the ridge thermals most afternoons — a 3m wingspan is normal. Langurs and rhesus macaques work the café stretch; keep food zipped. Gaddi shepherd dogs guard flocks above Snowline in summer — give herds a wide berth and let the shepherd call the dogs. Himalayan black bears are rare but recorded in the oak band in autumn; noise on the trail at dawn/dusk is the standard precaution. Leopard sightings near Dharamkot are occasional and nocturnal — another reason not to descend after dark.

## Camping at Triund

The meadow campsite (2,870m) sits below the top; the Forest Rest House nearby is basic and often unstaffed. Since 2019 the Forest Department has periodically restricted overnight camping on the meadow — check the current rule at the Galu forest check-post (small fee ledger, usually staffed till 17:00). Cafés at Triund and Snowline rent tents and blankets in season. Nights are windy; pitch low on the leeward east side.

## Indrahar Pass extension

Beyond Triund the route continues to Laka Got (3,350m) and the Lahesh Cave bivouac (3,600m), then the steep gully scramble to Indrahar Pass (4,342m) — a serious 2-day extension requiring a guide in most conditions, crampons outside high summer, and no phone signal anywhere past Laka Got. Do not attempt it as a day-extension from Triund on a whim.

## Phone signal and offline reality

Jio and Airtel hold patchy 4G to about Galu; above Magic View the signal dies in the forest shadow. Triund Top gets weak opportunistic signal on clear days when facing the valley — enough for SMS, rarely for calls. Galu Temple is the last dependable point to send a message; that is why it is the SOS rally point.

## Emergency practice

For rescue, the effective chain is: nearest humans on trail → café staff (they have radios/local numbers) → Galu check-post → SDRF Dharamshala. Helicopter evacuation from the meadow happens only in daylight and calm wind. Mark your last known position code, send it via any passing trekker if you must stay put, and stay on the main trail — every recorded Triund fatality involved leaving the path or descending after dark.

## Night trekking

Headlamps are mandatory kit even for a day plan; the forest section goes fully dark 30 minutes after sunset. The trail is navigable at night only to experienced walkers — the 22 curves section has drop-offs that claim ankles. If sunset beats you above Magic View, the safe calls are: sleep at Snowline café, or descend only to Magic View and wait for moonrise.

## Altitude and pacing

Triund's 2,875m rarely causes altitude sickness, but the 800m gain from McLeod Ganj in ~4 hours is enough for headaches if you skip water. Naismith with the standard correction — 5 km/h plus 10 min per 100m of ascent — matches fit trekkers; add 25% for a relaxed pace with photo stops. Most parties take 3.5–5 hours up, 2.5–3 hours down.

## Culture and etiquette

Galu Devi temple marks the trailhead ridge — the veranda is a traditional rain shelter, keep it clean. The Gaddi shepherds you meet above Snowline run flocks on ancestral summer routes; a "namaste" and space for the herd is the etiquette. Carry your trash down — the Mountain Cleaners collective runs collection points at Dharamkot; the meadow has no waste removal.
